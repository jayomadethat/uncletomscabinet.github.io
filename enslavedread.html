Enslaved Child Reads Word, South Declares It 'Witchcraft'

By Charles Worthington III | May 27, 1852

In what authorities are calling “an unholy display of eyes meeting letters,” 9-year-old Josiah, a young field laborer on the Pickens Plantation, allegedly read the word “light” from a discarded Bible page while alone in the barn. Witnesses say the barn glowed faintly for several seconds — or perhaps that was just the kerosene lamp.

The boy’s ability to sound out syllables sent shockwaves through the community, with local pastor Reverend Amos Hardgristle declaring, “If God wanted him to read, He would’ve sent him to Yale. Or at the very least, given him boots.”

“First they read, then they think, and next thing you know, they’re forming opinions — and we can’t have that.”
— Local Councilman

In response, a town emergency meeting was held, during which a ban on all nouns longer than four letters was passed unanimously. Efforts are now underway to confiscate books, pamphlets, worded boxes, and anything bearing the cursed Latin alphabet.

“We’re not saying it’s witchcraft,” said plantation foreman Thomas P. Houndstooth, “but we’re also not *not* saying it.” He later added, “We believe the boy may have been possessed by Northern ideas.”

Josiah has since been relocated to the “field of re-education,” a mysterious plot of land coincidentally shaped like a whipping post. Meanwhile, plans are in place to replace all schoolbooks with corn husks and verbal repetition da phrase, “the South is always right.”

Whether this moment marks a shift in Southern history or is simply another case of a child exercising dangerously independent thought remains to be seen. But one thing is certain: never again shall literacy be mistaken for liberty.
