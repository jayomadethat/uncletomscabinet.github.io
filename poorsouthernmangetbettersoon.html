Southern Man SHOCKED after being subject to "less than human” treatment.

By Joan D. Coughefeller | July 1, 1852

Mr. Bartholomew Cripps, a self-proclaimed connoisseur of foreign manners, returned this week from a pilgrimage to Mecca with urgent warnings for his neighbors. According to local reports, Cripps spent several weeks abroad, during which he was compelled to pick his own crops and wash his own garments — an ordeal he insists was “entirely unbefitting a gentleman of West Virginia.”

Neighbors nodded solemnly, relieved to reaffirm their belief that labor is only respectable when performed by someone else.

“Tea was made at once for Mr. Cripps upon his return,” reported one acquaintance. “He was not expected to fetch it himself, of course. There are other people for that”

Upon hearing the horror stories, the Town Judge felt physically ill. He had to rest for a few hours, occasionally muttering, "Why would a man do that?", a few times in his sleep.

Meanwhile, the enslaved individuals who had been forced to serve Mr. Cripps abroad reportedly expressed sympathy for his plight, though Cripps could sense bouts of laughter behind his back. He was right.
