Slave Breaks Toe, Delays Harvest

By Ulysses the Pauper | May 30, 1852

At Magnolia Grove Plantation, chaos erupted Tuesday morning after a field hand reportedly stubbed his toe on an obstinate cotton root.

Eyewitnesses describe the incident as “an act of sheer defiance by vegetable matter,” noting that harvest operations came to a halt for a full six minutes — long enough for several overseers to reconsider the morality of planting anything at all.”

“Seen below is the Crime Scene, Investigators are still looking into what sparked this rebellion.”

In a dramatic response, the plantation leadership has issued new regulations never seen before: all field hands must wear reinforced boots, and any cotton root exceeding three inches in height must submit to daily obedience drills. Rumors suggest a task force is being formed to patrol for insubordinate flora.

The workers reacted strangely to receiving the reinforced boots. Many were oddly seen pricking their fingers and other extremities on other cotton roots. They were promptly given more reinforced material. Overseer Hamliton wondered what caused them to act so strange.

While the bloody north seems to downpour the significance of this six-minute delay, Magnolia Grove officials remain steadfast: no foot, root, or leaf shall defy the efficiency of Southern agriculture again, "Not even the North Themselves."
